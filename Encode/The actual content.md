**What technical project / achievement am I most proud of and why?**

I taught myself deep learning by reading a publication and applying someone else's methodology on a different dataset. Now, as a data scientist, I train ML and DL models from scratch, deploy them into production, monitor data and model drift, and communicate insights to technical and non-technical stakeholders. I am most proud of the level of scientific rigor I bring to model explainability in decision-critical domains.

The paper I copied used autoencoders to fill gaps in lunar surface temperature observations across latitudes. The moon is relatively simple environment so we can theoretically model surface temperature deterministically. However, when we delegate such a task to a black box, there is a requirement for explainability because physical science is governed by equations, laws, and relationships that must be preserved. When I retrained the model on a dataset containing craters, I found that I was probing the generalizability of the architecture to more complex environments and features. And when I found that the model's latent space looked vastly different from the paper, even though the performance was comparable, I learned that a dataset can be carefully selected to encourage a model to learn defensible things.

In a data-rich domain, data curation draws the model's attention to features that may have been lost in the noise and sparsity of real data. From a mathematical standpoint, deep learning is about finding the most influential signals in a dataset but explainability is about finding out if there are real-life defensible arguments for the signals that are identified.

**Do I have any experience working on cross-disciplinary teams?**

I have been engaging the London Borough of Islington as a consultant on a project about social choice. I work on a cross-disciplinary team of residents, councillors and decision-makers to design resident engagement tools that better reflect group sentiment, preference, and disappointment. Our goal is to balance the user-friendliness of data collection with the level of insight that is required to make fair and defensible decisions that affect residents of the borough.

Social choice theory is a rich mathematical field that I have been reading about with a friend. This project was our initiative. We wanted to measure real-world fairness across different ways of aggregating possibly conflicting preferences into collective choices. Everyone is largely familiar with voting systems and their flaws so we worked with the council to formalize these intuitions for the decision-makers and residents. In resident focus groups, we measured preference across the same suite of alternatives using different methods, ranging from the familiar dot voting, preference ranking, and Likert scales to more complex representations of liquid democracy. We are now working with the council to map algorithmic divergence to actual decision-making, where my primary objective is to express fairness and disappointment of individuals and the collective in a way that informs action.

This proactive feedback loop I established early on with the residents is a learning from a past project. In the Singapore Government, I was embedded in a team of policy-makers, economists, and career coaches, building a model to predict jobseeker employability given their resume, employment history, demographics, and live snapshots of the job market. We wanted to design and target interventions to proactively help the jobseekers that might struggle the most. In this project, I struggled to connect key data insights to decision-making. Policy-makers couldn't justify the optics of interventions related to race and sex, which I found were statistically strong predictors of employability. And we couldn't design up-skilling programs for jobseekers when their skills were not necessarily the problem.

A cross-disciplinary team's disagreements are honest tests of whether we are solving the right problem. I started the Islington project with that learning, working with residents and letting their vocabulary shape the questions. Human context is a prior that I, as a data scientist, bear the responsibility to probe.
